Newton’s Laws of Motion

  • First Law (Inertia): An object in motion stays in motion unless acted on by an external force.
  • Second Law (F = ma): Force equals mass times acceleration.
  • Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

These laws describe how things move and interact with forces. Think of seat belts in cars or rockets launching — Newton's laws explain them all.

The Law of Universal Gravitation

Newton proposed that gravity is a force that pulls all objects toward one another. His formula:

F = G * (m1 * m2) / r²

This means that the force between two masses decreases with the square of the distance between them. It's why the Moon orbits Earth and why apples fall to the ground.

Impact of His Discoveries

Newton's work united the heavens and Earth under the same laws. His theories helped explain planetary motion, build machines, launch satellites, and laid the foundation for classical physics.

He built on the work of Galileo and later inspired Einstein. His laws remain fundamental in engineering and science today.

Original Works

Newton’s most famous publication is Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687), where he explained gravity and motion mathematically.

Read it online via The Newton Project.

About Isaac Newton

Born in 1643, Newton was an English mathematician, physicist, and astronomer. He formulated laws that changed our understanding of nature. He died in 1727 and is buried at Westminster Abbey.
